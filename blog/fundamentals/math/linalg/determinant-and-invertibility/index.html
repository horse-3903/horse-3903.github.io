Determinant and Invertibility

Math fundamentals note for IOAI ML preparation.

19 February 2026

Syllabus Map

  • Study map: Syllabus Study Map

Core Idea

  • Determinant describes signed area/volume scaling of a linear map.
  • Invertibility means a unique reverse mapping exists.

What Determinant Means

  • Think of a matrix AAA as a transformation of space.
  • ∣det⁡(A)∣\lvert \det(A) \rvert∣det(A)∣ tells how much AAA scales area (in 2D) or volume (in 3D).
    • ∣det⁡(A)∣=3\lvert \det(A)\rvert = 3∣det(A)∣=3 means areas/volumes become 3 times larger.
    • ∣det⁡(A)∣=0.2\lvert \det(A)\rvert = 0.2∣det(A)∣=0.2 means they shrink to one-fifth.
  • The sign of det⁡(A)\det(A)det(A) tells orientation:
    • det⁡(A)>0\det(A) > 0det(A)>0: orientation preserved.
    • det⁡(A)<0\det(A) < 0det(A)<0: orientation flipped (reflection involved).
  • det⁡(A)=0\det(A)=0det(A)=0 means at least one dimension is collapsed, so information is lost.
  • Geometric picture:
    • In 2D, AAA maps the unit square to a parallelogram.
    • In 3D, AAA maps the unit cube to a parallelepiped.
    • The area/volume of that transformed shape is exactly ∣det⁡(A)∣\lvert\det(A)\rvert∣det(A)∣.

What Invertibility Means

  • AAA is invertible if every output comes from exactly one input.
  • Equivalent statements:
    • Columns of AAA are linearly independent.
    • rank⁡(A)\operatorname{rank}(A)rank(A) is full.
    • The equation Ax=bAx=bAx=b has a unique solution for every bbb.
  • If AAA is not invertible (singular), multiple inputs can map to the same output, so no unique reverse exists.
  • Geometric picture:
    • If AAA flattens space (for example, a plane to a line), different points collapse to the same output.
    • Once points overlap after transformation, a unique reverse map is impossible.
  • Quick visual test in 2D:
    • Compute Ae1Ae_1Ae1​ and Ae2Ae_2Ae2​ (images of basis vectors).
    • If they are collinear, transformed area is zero ⇒det⁡(A)=0\Rightarrow \det(A)=0⇒det(A)=0 and AAA is not invertible.
    • If they are not collinear, transformed area is nonzero ⇒det⁡(A)≠0\Rightarrow \det(A)\ne 0⇒det(A)=0 and AAA is invertible.

Key Formulas

For 2×22\times22×2 matrix:

A=[abcd],det⁡(A)=ad−bcA=\begin{bmatrix}a & b\\ c & d\end{bmatrix},\quad \det(A)=ad-bcA=[ac​bd​],det(A)=ad−bc A−1A=I,det⁡(A)≠0⇔A invertibleA^{-1}A = I, \quad \det(A)\ne 0 \Leftrightarrow A \text{ invertible}A−1A=I,det(A)=0⇔A invertible

Quick Example

A=[1224]A=\begin{bmatrix}1 & 2\\ 2 & 4\end{bmatrix}A=[12​24​]
  • Here, det⁡(A)=1⋅4−2⋅2=0\det(A)=1\cdot4-2\cdot2=0det(A)=1⋅4−2⋅2=0.
  • The second row is a multiple of the first, so the matrix collapses 2D space onto a line.
  • Therefore AAA is singular and has no inverse.

Practical Notes

Determinant near zero implies numerical instability.

  • Systems become ill-conditioned even before exact singularity.

Avoid explicit inverse when solving systems.

  • Prefer stable solvers (solve, factorisation methods).

Why This Matters for ML

  • Invertibility and conditioning explain why some regression problems are numerically unstable.
  • Near-singular matrices produce large parameter swings from small data perturbations.
  • Regularised objectives (for example Ridge) improve conditioning by shifting spectra.
  • Determinant-based terms also appear in Gaussian likelihoods and normalising transformations.
