Setup

Definition:

Sequential Entry-Deterrence Game

  • Players: Entrant and Incumbent.
  • Strategies: Entrant chooses Out or Enter; Incumbent chooses Accommodate or Fight after entry.
  • Rules: Players move sequentially and optimal play is found by backward induction.

Game Tree

Payoff Details

  • If Out: (3,0)(3,0)(3,0).
  • If Enter and Accommodate: (2,2)(2,2)(2,2).
  • If Enter and Fight: (1,−1)(1,-1)(1,−1).

Derivation (Backward Induction)

  • If entry occurs, incumbent compares 222 (accommodate) vs 111 (fight), so chooses Accommodate.

  • Anticipating accommodation, entrant compares 222 (enter) vs 000 (out), so chooses Enter.

Nash Equilibrium

Result:

The subgame perfect Nash equilibrium strategy profile is:

{P1,P2}↦{Enter,Accommodate}\{ \text{P1}, \text{P2} \} \mapsto \{ \text{Enter}, \text{Accommodate} \}{P1,P2}↦{Enter,Accommodate}

The equilibrium outcome payoffs are (2,2)(2,2)(2,2).

Nash Equilibrium vs.\ SPNE

AccommodateFight
Enter2, 2-1, 1
Out0, 30, 3
  • The strategy profile {Out,Fight} is also a Nash equilibrium.
    • Given Fight after entry, the entrant prefers Out because 0>−10>-10>−1.
    • Given Out, the incumbent’s action after entry is off the equilibrium path, so deviating from Fight to Accommodate does not change the realised payoff.
    • Hence no player gains from a unilateral deviation at that strategy profile, so Nash equilibrium allows {Out,Fight}.
  • But it is not subgame perfect because, in the subgame after Enter, the incumbent prefers Accommodate since 2>12>12>1.

Result:

The associated equilibria are:

  • {P1,P2}↦{Enter,Accommodate}\{\text{P1}, \text{P2} \} \mapsto \{ \text{Enter}, \text{Accommodate} \}{P1,P2}↦{Enter,Accommodate} with a payoff of (2,2)(2,2)(2,2).
  • {P1,P2}↦{Out,Fight}\{\text{P1}, \text{P2} \} \mapsto \{ \text{Out}, \text{Fight} \}{P1,P2}↦{Out,Fight} with a payoff of (0,3)(0,3)(0,3).

Only {Enter,Accommodate} is SPNE.

Insights

Insight:

A threat to Fight may appear in a Nash equilibrium even when it is not credible.

SPNE removes such equilibria by requiring optimal play in every subgame, not just along the realised path.
