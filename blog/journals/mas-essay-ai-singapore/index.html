Executive Summary

The age of artificial intelligence, driven by plummeting inference costs and model performance, is transforming Singapore’s industries, workforce and competitive advantage. Singapore is favourably positioned to thrive in the age of AI, especially due to its knowledge-based economy, forward-looking government and world-class labour force. However, Singapore will need to balance these productivity gains while managing disruptions and ensuring inclusive growth.

In Chapter 1, we introduce the concepts of artificial intelligence and contextualise it to Singapore’s economic structure.

In Chapter 2, we analyse how AI reshapes Singapore’s economic landscape across three dimensions. First, AI reshapes Singapore’s industries by shifting them towards knowledge-intensive production in its four leading sectors, simultaneously expanding productive capacity and altering cost structures. Second, AI restructures labour by augmenting complex, uniquely human tasks and automating routine cognitive tasks, driving wage growth and creating new forms of human expertise. Third, AI reconfigures Singapore’s national competitive advantage through hub-based agglomeration, strategic positioning along the global AI value chain, and institutional coordination that allows Singapore to serve as a global hub in AI.

In Chapter 3, we examine how AI can be used to mitigate the impacts of disruptions and inequality that is inherent in AI adoption. AI can effectively target transitionary costs like frictional unemployment and demand-pull inflationary pressures, as well as ensure inclusive growth through targeted SME empowerment and AI-driven upskilling.

Overall, we conclude that Singapore’s long-term growth advantage stems from its capacity for institutional orchestration across the country, firms and labour markets. Singapore’s government-led coordination accelerates AI integration, strengthens its strategic positioning, and manages the frictions inherent in doing so. However, delays in workforce reskilling or uneven SME adoption risk bifurcating the labour market. It is ultimately the quality of institutional action which determines whether AI’s gains are broadly distributed or narrowly concentrated.

1. Introduction

The emergence of Artificial Intelligence (AI),1 as the next General-Purpose Technology (GPT)2 is set to become the cornerstone of Singapore’s future economic strategy, fundamentally reshaping our economy.

1.1 The AI-Driven Economy

AI is expected to accelerate Singapore’s transition to a productivity-led growth economy.3 Economic growth is primarily driven by capital, labour and technological innovation.4 Singapore has historically relied on an extensive growth model5, driven by strong capital6 and labour accumulation7 to power its economy.

Figure 1.1.0a. Singapore’s firm pace of GDP growth over the past decade stemmed from a combination of strong capital accumulation and to a lesser extent, Total Factor Productivity (TFP) gains.8

However amidst declining fertility rate,9 a super-ageing population,10 and tightened foreign labour policy,11 sustaining Singapore’s economic growth will depend on productivity-led growth driven by AI.

Figure 1.1.0b. Singapore Resident and Non-Resident Labour Growth.12

Figure 1.1.0c. The Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR) of residents aged 15 years and over has been declining since 2021. LFPR is expected to continue trending downward as more post-war baby boomers progressively age. The decline is likely to persist until the ageing population profile stabilises.13

By strategically positioning itself as an early mover in the global AI race, Singapore can secure a competitive edge in the new era of technological revolution where economic advantages no longer depend on a country’s workforce size, but on their ability to efficiently organise human capital with AI.

2. Analysis

2.1 AI Reshaping the Industry

2.1.1 From Service Execution to Knowledge Creation

AI accelerates the shift towards a knowledge-based economy not just by automating individual tasks, but by displacing service execution as the primary source of value creation, moving industries up the value chain toward knowledge-intensive production.14

Figure 2.1.1a. The gradual shift in workforce towards PMET jobs.

While AI performs within service execution,15 knowledge creation requires constructing this problem-space and identifying what question to ask where no prior template exists.

Figure 2.1.1b. Singapore’s National AI Strategy 2.0 targets AI deployment across four Leading Economic Sectors and three Smart Nation Priorities.16

Singapore’s established depth in finance, maritime trade, and biomedical sciences generates the domain-specific data and institutional trust that AI deployment at scale requires.17 These sectors become the training ground for high-value, regionally differentiated AI applications that economies with less institutional depth cannot replicate, provided Singapore’s workforce concentrates at the knowledge-creation layer rather than the execution layer.18

2.1.2 Expansion of Productive Capacity and Cost Restructuring

AI expands Singapore’s productive capacity by augmenting labour and capital simultaneously,19 relaxing the binding factor constraints of a resource- and labour-scarce economy. With plummeting inference costs, AI restructures cost economics across Singapore’s four strategic sectors.20

Figure 2.1.2a. The AI Price-Performance Inversion: as compute costs fall and model capabilities rise, AI transitions from an enterprise luxury to a commodity input.21

This is highlighted in Singapore’s key industries. In manufacturing, AI-driven process control raises output per unit capital and reduces downtime for precision manufacturing.22 In Financial Services, MAS’s Project Mindforge has accelerated AI deployment by spearheading frameworks for AI usage.23 In Biomedical Sciences, ASTAR is deploying AI to accelerate drug research, such as the TISHUMAP project.24 Across Transport and Logistics, PSA’s Tuas Port has deployed AI-powered traffic management and autonomous guided vehicles to achieve a record 44.66mil TEUs in 2025.25

Figure 2.1.2b. Gartner forecasts that the cost of GenAI inference will continue falling due to the advancement of AI, resulting in firms having near-zero marginal costs.26

Once models are deployed, the marginal costs of operation approach zero, shifting production towards fixed capital investment. However, this fixed-cost structure raises the minimum efficient scale for firms, creating a natural barrier to entry that risks entrenching market concentration.27 For Singapore’s SME-dominated economy, this risks entrenching a two-tier economy where productivity gains accrue exclusively to large incumbents.28

2.2 AI Reshaping the Workforce

Amidst an ageing population, declining fertility rate and tightened foreign manpower policies. TFP, rather than labour force expansion, is key to sustaining output growth.

2.2.1 Task Composition in AI-Augmented Workforce

Occupations can be visualised as a bundle of tasks, some of which can be automated or augmented by AI.29 30

Figure 2.2.1a. Autor, Levy, and Murnane Model of Labor categories. Tasks with higher degrees of repetition and manual work are more prone to AI automation.

AI augmentation31 in Singapore’s workforce frees workers from routine tasks and reallocates efforts towards complex problem solving and decision making tasks requiring more cognition, leading to higher workforce productivity in line with the Acemoglu-Loebbing model.

Figure 2.2.1b. The Acemoglu-Loebbing model maps tasks (ordered by complexity), to workers (ordered by skill level). A range of tasks of intermediate complexity is taken over by AI while tasks at the extremes of the distribution are performed by workers.32

However, AI performs poorly in tasks with high EPOCH scores.33 34 For such tasks, AI can only complement labour as their non-codifiable and context-sensitive nature makes full automation limited.35

Given the PMET-heavy nature of Singapore’s economy,36 37 AI augmentation in PMET-intensive sectors with high EPOCH scores is likely to augment 76% of workers. This significantly outweighs the negative effects of job displacements in industries with low EPOCH scores like manufacturing38 projected to affect <9% of workers.39

Figure 2.2.1c. The figure shows the distribution and ranking of occupations by EPOCH Score. Panel A shows the EPOCH score distribution of all detailed occupations in O*NET’s40 major occupational group. Panel B shows selected occupations ranked from the highest EPOCH score from top to bottom. EPOCH framework is used to capture human capabilities that complement, rather than substitute, artificial intelligence41.

AI can expand human capabilities and make existing human skills and expertise more valuable, resulting in task creation. This creates new forms of expertises which expands the set of tasks performed by workers rather than by machines and raises the labor share of value-added.42 43

An AI-augmented workforce shifts jobs toward non-routine, high-value tasks requiring human judgment, increasing productivity and demand for higher-skilled roles.

2.2.2 Labour Productivity and Innovation

AI-augmentation raises labour productivity and accelerates the rate of innovation.

Figure 2.2.2a. Improved efficiency and enhanced creativity was amongst the top few benefits perceived by AI-using workers in 2025.44

AI augmentation is expected to unlock >55% more labour productivity gains within organisations.45 46 In addition, AI lowers the fixed cost of R&D experimentation, compressing development cycles and raising the rate of innovation.47 Economically, AI is estimated to create S$198 billion in value for corporations by 2030.48

2.2.3 Wage Growth

AI augmentation allows workers to focus on higher value tasks with greater earning power, leading to estimated higher wages of >8%.49 50

Figure 2.2.3a. Increase in labour productivity was tied to an increase in real mean gross monthly income.51

2.2.4 Skills and Expertise

AI automation and augmentation changes the skillsets and expertise required in a workforce.

Figure 2.2.4a. Shares of employees who consider the stated skills to be core skills for the workforce amidst an AI-augmented workforce landscape.52

Corporations have adopted skill-based hiring practices and focus less on academic qualifications.53 This pace of skill change is even faster in AI-exposed jobs – 66% faster than in other roles.54

Figure 2.2.4b. Proportion of Job Vacancies For Which Academic Qualifications Were Not The Main Consideration55

This signals a shift in workforce skills and expertise to not only focus more on human core competencies,56 but also adaptability in an ever-evolving world. With employers now expecting workers to continuously upskill themselves to increase the value-added of their labour, 39% of workers’ core skills are expected to change by 2030.

Figure 2.2.4c. Evolution in the share of workers’ core skills expected to change and to remain the same within the next five years, 2016-2025.57

Figure 2.2.4d. With the government pushing for a “lifelong learning” mindset, the average training participation rate in 2025 rose to 54.8%.58

Figure 2.2.4e. Increased training participation rate in Singapore amidst an increasingly AI-augmented workforce.59

2.3 AI Reshaping Competitive Advantage

Classically, Porter’s Diamond Model holds that national competitive advantage is driven by factor conditions, demand sophistication, industrial clustering, and firm rivalry.60 AI reconfigures all four determinants simultaneously, constructing a new competitive advantage through endogenous means.

Figure 2.3a. Singapore’s AI Readiness Index ranking, reflecting its institutional infrastructure and talent pipeline relative to global peers.61

2.3.1 Hub-based Agglomeration Effects

Figure 2.3.1a. The World’s Leading AI Investment Hubs. 62

AI intensifies agglomeration economies through three mechanisms: data exchange,63 talent pooling,64 and cross-category innovation.65 What distinguishes AI from prior technologies is that data is non-rivalous,66 enabling cluster-wide pooling without exclusion costs. These effects are also self-reinforcing, as better clusters attract more data and create stronger models.

Figure 2.3.1b. Processes of knowledge spillovers in entrepreneurship due to AI.67

Singapore’s unique economic composition provides a reservoir of domain-specific data that deepens its advantage. The ESR’s goal of positioning Singapore as the preferred location to host AI firms directly targets this flywheel by reducing the regulatory uncertainty and infrastructure gaps,68 while NAIS 2.0’s placemaking enabler aims to deepen Singapore’s attractiveness as a hub for global AI talent and firms.69

2.3.2 Global Value Chain Positioning

Figure 2.3.2a. Nvidia’s proposed ‘5-Layer Cake’ model for understanding AI value chains.70

AI restructures value capture along the global stack, allowing economies to target specific layers rather than entire industries.

Figure 2.3.2b. Current and Planned Capacity of Data Centres.71

At Layer 1 (energy), Singapore lacks the resources to remain competitive. At Layer 2 (chips), Singapore does not compete at the forefront of GPU production, but is a critical global node in the semiconductor supply chain.72 73

At Layer 3 (infrastructure), Singapore is already a major regional data centre hub hosting hyperscaler operations with planned capacity expansions reinforcing this position.74 75

At Layers 4–5 (models and applications), Singapore’s sectoral depth in finance, logistics, and biomedical sciences positions itself to provide high-value domain-specific AI deployment. As such, Singapore can capture value by building models that embed industry-specific workflows and regulatory knowledge into sectors it already dominates.76

2.3.3 Institutional Coordination and Geopolitical Neutrality

AI deployment requires a harmonisation across data infrastructure, regulation, talent, and industry, making institutional coordination a source of competitive advantage in itself.

Figure 2.3.3a. Singapore’s National AI Strategy 2.0.

Singapore’s governance architecture operates as a mutually reinforcing system. NAIS 2.0 organises this across three systems: Activity Drivers, People and Communities, and Infrastructure and Environment.77 The PDPA and Model AI Governance Framework provides compliance clarity, while MAS Regulatory Sandboxes create structured spaces for financial innovation.78 These reduce regulatory uncertainty and make Singapore’s effective governance a unique value proposition to MNCs.79

Figure 2.3.3b. The intensification of the US-China Chip War.80

Geopolitically, US-China tensions in semiconductor trade have polarised the global AI landscape.81 Singapore’s active non-alignment capitalises on this, hosting both US and Chinese technology firms and functioning as a neutral compute hub for all countries and preserving the optionality of firms.82 That said, pressures from either side could force alignment towards one faction which collapses this source of competitive advantage.83

3. Evaluation

3.1 AI Managing Disruptions

3.1.1 Transitionary Costs of AI Adoption

Figure 3.1.1a. The Productivity J-curve illustrates the “Productivity Paradox” in adoption of AI. Performance initially dips due to transitionary costs (in blue). However, stakeholders expect immediate gains in productivity (in red). With effective management, disruption can be minimised and long-term benefits can be achieved quicker (in green).84

AI adoption necessitates a productivity J-curve, where productivity initially declines due to transitionary costs before long-run efficiency gains materialise.85 At the same time, AI causes frictional unemployment: employers face a smaller candidate pool for specific AI-augmented roles, while workers are unaware of the new roles in the new workforce.

The Singaporean economy’s service-dominant structure means frictional unemployment concentrates in occupations undergoing AI-augmentation, disproportionately affecting mid-career professionals in routine cognitive roles.86 87

Figure 3.1.1b. Data from LinkedIn’s 2025 Workplace Learning Report illustrates how career development champions leverage AI data to identify skill gaps and create skills-based career paths at a significantly higher rate than other respondents.88

AI directly addresses both sides of this transition. For workers, AI career navigation tools flag skill adjacencies, reducing uncertainty in job search.89 On the firm side, process-mining algorithms accelerate the firm’s descent through the J-curve by streamlining workflow transitions.

Singapore’s Career and Skills Passport infrastructure, operated under SkillsFuture, provides foundational data interoperability for such matching systems.90 When integrated with AI-driven competency mapping, this platform can identify all skill-adjacency pathways without complete retraining, reducing worker-job mismatches.91

3.1.2 Demand-Pull Inflation

Figure 3.1.2a. Inflationary effects from the rise in aggregate demand due to investments without a reciprocated rise in productive capacity.

AI adoption generates demand-pull inflation due to a structural asymmetry: AI investments raise aggregate demand immediately, while the rise in productive capacity is delayed.92 93

To combat this, AI can operate as a disinflationary force through supply-side expansion. By raising labour productivity faster than wages, it reduces unit labour costs, lowering firms’ marginal cost of production and exerting downward pressure on prices. The simultaneous rise in labour productivity, collapse in unit labour cost growth, and sharp decline in MAS Core Inflation reflects a positive supply shock, where cost per unit output falls even as production expands.94 By aligning investment timelines with productive capacity expansion, Singapore minimises the lag between demand growth and supply response, structurally anchoring price stability.

3.2 AI Ensuring Inclusive Growth

Inclusive growth is economic growth that is distributed fairly across society and creates opportunities for all. With AI expected to create $198 billion in economic value by 2030, it is imperative that AI is used to ensure inclusive growth.

Figure 3.2.0a. Singapore GINI Coefficient. A GINI Coefficient of 0 represents perfect equality while 1 represents perfect inequality.95

3.2.1 Upskilling of low-skilled workers

AI can better upskill low-skilled workers, equipping them with the necessary skills and resources to move up the value chain. AI identifies specific skill gaps in an individual’s profile and creates highly personalised training paths. In Singapore, tools like AI Career Coach identify resume gaps and recommend targeted courses from platforms like NTUC LearningHub to help workers transition into in-demand roles.96

AI can also provide low-skilled workers with “tacit knowledge” that was previously only gained through years of experience,97 boosting their productivity by an estimated 34%.98

The effect of the AI upskilling of low-skilled workers in a high-complementarity, high-productivity AI scenario, would allow for an inclusive growth that benefits all workers99, not just high-skilled ones.

Figure 3.2.1a. Change in Total Income by Income Percentile.
The panels show three scenarios from the model: (1) low complementarity, (2) high complementarity, and (3) high complementarity and high productivity. The panels show the change in total income by income percentile, decomposed into the change in labor income in blue and the change in capital income in orange. Source: IMF staff calculations.

3.2.2 Empowering SMEs

AI ensures inclusive growth for SMEs by enabling them to leverage AI to bridge productivity gaps with larger corporations. While only 14.5% of SMEs had adopted AI by 2024, the government and private sector have launched extensive frameworks to accelerate this transition100. These frameworks include the development of pre-approved, cost-effective AI solutions from reputable vendors101, with grant support to lower adoption barriers for SMEs, allowing SMEs to better reap the benefits of AI augmentation.

Figure 3.2.2a. Use of AI to empower SMEs.102

In financial management, AI automates tasks like bookkeeping and forecasting, freeing workers for strategic decision-making.103 In customer service, AI-powered chatbots improve customer interactions and streamline customer handling.104 Indubitably, AI has leveled the playing field for SMEs to compete with MNCs, ensuring inclusive growth.

4. Conclusion

AI represents a fundamental shift in Singapore’s economic paradigm, evolving from a supporting tool into a primary engine of growth. Singapore’s long-term growth advantage will not stem from AI adoption alone, but from its ability to institutionally orchestrate AI across country, firms, and labour markets, thereby internalising productivity gains while mitigating structural inequalities.

Unlike larger economies where market forces alone drive uneven diffusion, Singapore’s government-led coordination enables the alignment of industrial policy, human capital development, and firm-level adoption. In this sense, Singapore’s most durable competitive advantage may be the institutional capacity to govern transitions well.
